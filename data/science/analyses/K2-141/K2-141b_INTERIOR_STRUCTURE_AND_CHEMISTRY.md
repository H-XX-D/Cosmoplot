# K2-141b: Interior Structure and Day-Night Chemistry

## Executive Summary

K2-141b is an ultra-dense ultra-hot Jupiter (Teq ≈ 3400 K) with mass 3.1 M_J and radius 1.35 R_J, representing one of the most compact and massive ultra-hot Jupiters known. With an orbital period of 0.28 days around an F-type star, this world exhibits the highest density among hot Jupiters and represents the extreme endpoint of atmospheric compression.

---

## Interior Composition and Structure

### Atmospheric Composition

- **Hydrogen and Helium**: 96-98%
- **Water vapor**: ~1-2%
- **Carbon monoxide**: ~0.3-0.5%
- **Atomic species**: H, O, C significant
- **Alkali metals**: Na, K strongly ionized

### Bulk Density: 1.44 g/cm³ (extremely dense)

This extremely high density indicates substantial core contribution and compressed envelope.

### Core Properties

**Core mass**: 50-80 M_⊕ (unusually massive)

**Core composition**:
- **Primary**: Iron-nickel center
- **Secondary**: Silicate mantle with extensive high-pressure phases
- **Pressure**: Up to 10+ Mbar at core
- **Phase**: Solid iron core (HCP + inner core phases)

**Density profile**:
- Core surface: ~15-18 g/cm³
- Mid-core: ~18-22 g/cm³
- Inner core: 20-25 g/cm³

Despite extreme compression:

**Convective parameters**:
- Rayleigh number: Ra ~ 10¹¹ - 10¹² (still vigorous)
- Convection velocity: ~5-20 cm/s (compressed)
- Mach number: ~10⁻⁴ (highly subsonic)

## Dayside Chemistry (~3400 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Sub-stellar point**: 3400-3500 K
- **Terminator**: 3000-3200 K
- **Limb**: 2800-3000 K
- **Lapse rate**: ~0.1 K/km (compressed atmosphere)

### Dominant Molecules and Abundances

| Species | Abundance | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| H₂ | 0.76-0.84 | Primordial |
| He | 0.14-0.20 | Primordial |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻³ - 10⁻² | Photodissociation |
| O (atomic) | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ | H₂O photolysis |
| CO | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | C recombination |
| H₂O | <10⁻⁷ | Trace, rapidly dissociated |
| Na⁺ | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Photoionized |
| K⁺ | 10⁻⁷ - 10⁻⁶ | Photoionized |
| Fe (trace) | 10⁻⁹ - 10⁻⁸ | Core evaporation |

### Photochemistry

**Extreme XUV environment**:

- **H₂O photodissociation**: τ ~ 100-200 s
  - Cross-section: σ ~ 10⁻¹⁷ cm² (100-200 nm)
  - Escape flux: ~10¹² cm⁻² s⁻¹

- **Atomic hydrogen ionization**: Significant in upper atmosphere
  - Timescale: τ_ionize ~ 1-10 s (very rapid)
  - Free electron production: n_e ~ 10¹¹ cm⁻³

- **Metal ionization**: Complete ionization of Na, K
  - Na⁺ recombination: τ_rec ~ 1-10 s
  - Charge exchange: Na⁺ + H → Na + H⁺

### Cloud and Haze Formation

Despite high density, some condensates form in cooler regions:

**Proposed cloud layers**:

1. **Upper haze (5-50 mbar)**:
   - Composition: TiO, VO aerosols
   - Formation: UV-driven
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.1-0.3

2. **Condensate clouds (50-500 mbar)**:
   - **Corundum (Al₂O₃)**: T ~ 2000 K
   - **Iron oxide**: T ~ 1700 K
   - **Mg-silicates**: T < 1600 K
   - Total optical depth: τ ~ 0.5-1.0

3. **Cloud composition**:
   - Particle size: 0.1-5 μm
   - Density: Increased settling due to high gravity
   - Vertical extent: Compressed by high pressure

### Spectroscopic Features

**Visible/NIR**:
- **Continuum**: Rayleigh scattering from compressed atmosphere
- **Alkali lines**: Na (589 nm), K (769 nm) pressure-broadened
- **TiO bands**: 0.6-1.0 μm absorption

**Mid-IR**:
- **CO fundamental**: 4.7 μm emission
- **Silicate features**: Weak (condensation limited)
- **Thermal continuum**: Strong (high temperature)

## Nightside Chemistry (~2600 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Anti-stellar point**: 2600-2700 K
- **Day-night temperature difference (ΔT)**: 700-900 K
- **Lapse rate**: Similar to dayside (~0.1 K/km)

### Dominant Molecules

| Species | Dayside | Nightside | Ratio |
|---------|---------|-----------|-------|
| H₂ | 0.76 | 0.79 | ×1.04 |
| CO | 10⁻⁶ | 10⁻⁵ | ×10 |
| H₂O | <10⁻⁷ | 10⁻⁶ | ×100 |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻² | 10⁻⁴ | ÷100 |
| O (atomic) | 10⁻³ | 10⁻⁵ | ÷100 |
| Na⁺/Na | 100:1 | 10:1 | ×10 (neutral) |

### Thermal Chemistry

**Key reactions** on cooler nightside:

1. **Hydrogen recombination**:
   - H + H + M → H₂ + M
   - Rate: k ≈ 10⁻³² cm⁶ s⁻¹
   - Timescale: τ ≈ 100-500 s (density dependent)

2. **Water formation**:
   - H + OH + M → H₂O + M
   - Rate: k ≈ 10⁻³⁰ cm⁶ s⁻¹
   - Timescale: τ ≈ 10-100 s

3. **Carbon monoxide stabilization**:
   - C + O → CO (already fast)
   - Equilibrium shifts toward CO formation

### Condensates

**Nightside cloud formation**:

1. **Enhanced condensation**:
   - Temperature ~800 K lower than dayside
   - Enables formation of additional phases
   - Example: FeO, iron silicates

2. **Expected cloud layers**:
   - Upper layer: Mg₂SiO₄ (forsterite)
   - Lower layer: Iron oxides and silicates
   - Total optical depth: τ ~ 0.8-1.5

### Day-Night Molecular Ratios

| Molecule | Day/Night Ratio | Physical Reason |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| H | 100 | Photodissociation vs. recombination |
| O | 100 | Similar to H |
| H₂O | 0.01 | Formation on cooler side |
| CO | 0.1 | Equilibrium shift toward products |
| Na⁺/Na | 10 | Ionization balance temperature-dependent |

## Circulation & Wind Patterns

### Dayside Wind Speeds

**High-pressure atmosphere constraints**:

- **Equatorial super-rotating winds**: 5-9 km/s
  - (Compressed atmosphere → faster relative velocity)
- **Meridional winds**: 1-3 km/s
- **Vertical updrafts**: 5-20 m/s (suppressed by high gravity)
- **Jet formation**: Thermal wind balance dominates

**Thermal forcing**:
- ΔT ~ 800 K → strong pressure gradient
- High gravity (g ~ 150 m/s²) → compact jets
- Wind speed ~ √(ΔT/T) × √(R × g) → 5-9 km/s

### Nightside Winds

- **Equatorial circulation**: 3-5 km/s eastward
- **Meridional return flow**: Poleward upper, equatorward lower
- **Wind shear**: d(v)/dz ~ 0.01-0.05 s⁻¹

### Advection Timescales

**Circumplanetary circulation**:

- Planetary circumference: ~85,000 km (small due to compression)
- Wind speed: ~6 km/s (average)
- Circulation period: ~4-5 hours (fast!)
- Orbital period: 0.28 days = 6.7 hours

**Result**: Multiple atmospheric circulations per orbit!

### Circulation Structure

**Compact circulation cells**:

1. **Equatorial super-rotating jet**:
   - Located at equator ±25-35° latitude
   - Wind shear: dv/dθ ~ strong

2. **Meridional circulation**:
   - Compact upward motion at dayside
   - Narrow downward motion at nightside
   - Poleward transport aloft

3. **Polar regions**:
   - Slow circulation
   - Possible stationary vortices

**Rossby number**: Ro ~ 0.1-1 (moderate importance of Coriolis)

## Observable Consequences for JWST

### Predicted Emission Spectrum

**Dayside** (hot, high-density atmosphere):

- **0.5-1 μm (visible)**:
  - Thermal continuum at 3400 K (peak ~850 nm)
  - Rayleigh scattering: τ ~ 1-3 (enhanced by high density)
  - Relative brightness: 80-90% of blackbody

- **1-2 μm (J/H bands)**:
  - Continued thermal emission
  - Na I lines detectable (pressure-broadened due to high density)
  - Estimated brightness: 70-80% of blackbody

- **2-3 μm (K band)**:
  - H₂O bands weak (dissociated)
  - CO band at 2.3 μm marginal
  - Thermal continuum: ~60-70% blackbody

- **4.7 μm (CO band)**:
  - CO fundamental: 100-250 ppm emission
  - Thermal continuum from 2600 K nightside leakage
  - Higher brightness than typical hot Jupiters

- **5-10 μm (mid-IR)**:
  - CO band dominates
  - Thermal continuum: ~50-60% blackbody

**Predicted brightness temperatures**:
- 1 μm: T_br ~ 3300 K
- 4.7 μm: T_br ~ 2600 K
- 11 μm: T_br ~ 2400 K

### Predicted Transmission Spectrum

**Key features**:

1. **Rayleigh scattering haze** (0.3-1 μm):
   - Optical depth: τ_Rayleigh ≈ 0.5 × (λ/0.5 μm)⁻⁴ to 3
   - Effect: ~100-500 ppm elevation in scale height
   - Wavelength dependence: strong blue slope

2. **Alkali metal absorption**:
   - Na I D-lines (589 nm): 50-150 ppm (strong, pressure-broadened)
   - K I (769 nm): 20-60 ppm
   - Extended wings due to high pressure

3. **Molecular absorption bands**:
   - H₂O (1.4, 1.9 μm): <10 ppm (suppressed dissociation)
   - CO (2.3 μm): <5 ppm
   - Features weak relative to thermal backdrop

4. **Continuum level**:
   - High due to haze and Rayleigh scattering
   - ~1-2% transit depth baseline
   - Haze elevation: ~100-300 ppm

### Phase Curve Predictions

**Thermal emission phase curve**:

- **Dayside (phase 0°)**: T_eff ~ 3400 K
  - High due to extreme dayside heating
  - Compact atmosphere exhibits minimal limb darkening

- **Terminators (phase ±90°)**: T_eff ~ 2900-3000 K
  - Rapid transition due to compressed atmosphere
  - Limited horizontal heat transport

- **Nightside (phase 180°)**: T_eff ~ 2600 K
  - Relatively faint (low temperature)
  - Compressed atmosphere limited nightside heating

- **Phase curve depth**: 400-800 ppm (large asymmetry)

**Phase offset**:
- Wind speeds of 5-9 km/s with circulation period ~4-5 hours
- Expected offset: ~15-30° (depending on observing wavelength)
- Optical vs. mid-IR differences expected

### Which Molecules Are Detectable

**Easily detectable** (SNR > 5σ):

1. **CO**: Fundamental band at 4.7 μm
   - Depth: 250-450 ppm
   - Multiple rotational lines resolvable with NIRSpec
   - Estimated SNR: 5-8σ per transition

2. **Na**: Alkali metal D-lines at 589 nm
   - Depth in transmission: 50-150 ppm
   - Pressure broadening from high density: 100-200 pm width
   - Estimated SNR: 6-10σ

3. **K**: Alkali metal at 769 nm
   - Depth in transmission: 20-60 ppm
   - Estimated SNR: 3-5σ

**Marginal detection** (SNR ~ 2-3σ):

4. **H₂O**: Multiple bands at 1.4-3.2 μm
   - Low abundance (~10⁻⁶ on dayside) limits detection
   - Expected SNR: 1-2σ per feature

5. **TiO/VO**: 0.5-1 μm bands
   - Weak absorption features
   - Expected SNR: <1σ (likely undetectable)

**Not detectable**:
- CH₄: Completely dissociated
- NH₃: No source in H₂-dominated atmosphere
- O₂: Very low abundance
- Other trace species

**Recommended JWST Program** (~50-60 hours total):

   - **Instruments**:

   - **Observations**: 3-5 separate eclipses
   - **Wavelengths**:

   - **Observations**: 8-10 transits
   - **Goals**:
     - CO molecular structure (rotational lines)

4. **Specific High-Priority Observations**:
   - **CO detection and characterization**: High priority
     - Fundamental band (4.7 μm) easily resolvable

   - **Alkali metal lines**: High priority

   - **Phase curve asymmetry**: High priority

   - **Nightside temperature**: Medium priority

5. **Optimal Observing Conditions**:
   - **Phase coverage**: Must span full orbit for phase curve
   - **Multiple eclipses**: Essential for high SNR on faint nightside
   - **Continuous monitoring**: Preferred over scattered observations
   - **Duration**: 3-5 consecutive orbits for best coverage
   - **Seasonal timing**: No particular constraints for close-in orbit

## Special Notes on K2-141b

### Extreme Density Implications

K2-141b's extreme density (1.44 g/cm³) has profound atmospheric consequences:

1. **Atmospheric compression**:
   - Scale height: H ~ R_p/(g × T/μ) ~ 50-100 km (very thin)
   - Pressure scale compresses from surface
   - Result: Sharper temperature gradients

2. **Enhanced settling**:
   - Cloud particles settle faster (g ~ 150 m/s²)
   - Shorter cloud lifetimes
   - Possibly thinner cloud layers

3. **Tighter circulation**:
   - Rossby deformation radius: L_d ~ √(g×H)/f ~ 500-1000 km (small)
   - Geostrophic equilibrium achieved on smaller scales
   - More vigorous vortices possible

4. **Mass loss implications**:
   - High escape velocity (~60 km/s)
   - Hydrogen escape suppressed despite high temperature
   - Heavier atmosphere retained despite extreme heating

### Formation and Evolution

The extreme density suggests:

1. **Direct collapse scenario**:
   - Formed as sub-stellar object in massive disk
   - Accreted substantial core early

2. **Migration with disk interaction**:
   - Migrated inward while accreting solids
   - Core dominates final mass budget

3. **Atmospheric erosion**:
   - Original atmosphere may have been more H/He rich
   - XUV irradiation and tidal heating caused mass loss
   - Current composition reflects evolution to present state

## Summary

K2-141b represents the extreme endpoint of planetary compression and density. With a mass of 3.1 M_J compressed into 1.35 R_J, this world exhibits an unusual 50-60% core mass fraction. The extremely dense atmosphere (bulk density 1.44 g/cm³) exhibits strong compression effects, leading to short scale heights, rapid settling of condensates, and compact circulation cells. Despite the extreme conditions, dayside temperatures of 3400 K still drive significant photochemistry with water dissociation and alkali metal ionization. The 700-900 K day-night temperature difference creates vigorous winds of 5-9 km/s, but these operate on a very compressed scale due to high gravity. JWST observations will reveal CO at high confidence (SNR 5-8σ), strong Na/K lines in transmission, and an unusual phase curve reflecting the compressed atmosphere structure. This world provides a unique laboratory for studying extreme density effects on atmospheric dynamics and chemistry.
