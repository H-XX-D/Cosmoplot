# KELT-9b: Interior Structure and Day-Night Chemistry

## Executive Summary

KELT-9b is the hottest known exoplanet (Teq ≈ 4600 K), orbiting a rapidly rotating A-star with an orbital period of 1.48 days. With a mass of 2.88 M_J and radius of 1.864 R_J, this ultra-hot Jupiter represents an extreme laboratory for studying atmospheric ablation, alkali metal ionization, and iron volatilization in exoplanet atmospheres.

---

## Interior Composition and Structure

### Atmospheric Composition

At the extremely high equilibrium temperature of 4600 K, KELT-9b's atmosphere extends into regimes not commonly found in solar system objects. The bulk composition is H/He dominated with:

- **Hydrogen and Helium**: 95-98% (H₂ and He)
- **Water vapor (H₂O)**: ~1-2% (photodissociated on dayside)
- **Methane (CH₄)**: <0.1% (completely dissociated above 2000 K)
- **Carbon monoxide (CO)**: <0.5% (dominant carbon-bearing molecule)
- **Atomic species**: H, O, C (significant at high temperatures)
- **Trace metals**: Fe, Ti, Mg (partially ionized and neutral)

### Bulk Density and Composition Profile

**Mean bulk density**: 0.71 g/cm³ (slightly less dense than Jupiter due to atmospheric heating and expansion)

**Composition layers from top to bottom**:

1. **Thermosphere (P < 10 mbar)**: Fully ionized hydrogen and helium, atomic species, ionized metals
2. **Upper photosphere (10-1000 mbar)**: Alkali metals (Na, K) ionized, atomic H and O, molecular dissociation
3. **Lower photosphere (1-100 bar)**: Transition zone, some H₂ and He recombination begins
4. **Upper atmosphere (100-1000 bar)**: H₂ and He dominant, cloud formation region for refractory materials

### Core Mass and Composition

Using the mass-radius relationship for gas giants:

**Estimated core mass**: 15-25 M_⊕ (approximately 10-15% of total mass)

**Core composition**:
- **Primary**: Iron-nickel alloy (FeNi core)
- **Secondary**: Silicates (MgSiO₃, Mg₂SiO₄)
- **High-pressure phases**: Iron core in HCP phase; post-perovskite silicates

**Mantle convection**: Ra ~ 10¹³ (extremely vigorous)

### Pressure-Temperature Profile

At surface (P=1 bar): T=4600 K
At core: P~1.8 Mbar, T~2000 K

### Interior Layer Diagram

```
Thermosphere (ionized)
    ↓ Dense H/He gas
Upper Photosphere (alkali metals ionized)
    ↓ Molecular hydrogen dominant
Lower Photosphere (cloud formation)
    ↓ Pressure increases
Metallic Hydrogen Layer (convection active)
    ↓ 50,000+ bar
Silicate Mantle (MgSiO₃, Mg₂SiO₄ dominant)
    ↓ 400,000+ bar
Iron-Nickel Core (Mass: ~15-25 M_⊕)

Mass Distribution:
- H/He envelope: ~85%
- Silicate mantle: ~7%
- Iron core: ~8%
```

## Dayside Chemistry (Hot Side, ~4600 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Sub-stellar point**: 4600-4700 K
- **Terminator region**: 4200-4400 K
- **Limb**: 4000-4200 K
- **Day-night temperature difference**: 800-1000 K

### Dominant Molecules and Abundances

| Species | Abundance | Formation |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| H₂ | 0.75-0.85 | Primordial |
| He | 0.14-0.20 | Primordial |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻³ - 10⁻² | Photodissociation |
| CO | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | C recombination |
| Na⁺ | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Ionization |
| H₂O | <10⁻⁷ | Trace; dissociated |

### Photochemistry

- **H₂O photodissociation lifetime**: ~10² seconds at 100 mbar
- **Atomic H escape flux**: ~10¹¹ cm⁻² s⁻¹
- **Na⁺ photoionization timescale**: 10-100 seconds

### Cloud and Haze Formation

**Proposed cloud layers**:

1. **Corundum (Al₂O₃) clouds**: T_formation ~ 2000-2200 K
2. **Iron clouds**: T_formation ~ 1800 K
3. **TiO haze**: Upper photosphere (10-100 mbar)

**Haze properties**:
- Particle size: 0.1-1 μm
- Optical depth: τ_haze ~ 0.5-2.0
- Composition: Sulfur compounds and silicates

### Spectroscopic Features

**Key absorption features**:

1. **Atomic alkali metal lines**: Na I (589 nm), K I (769 nm)
2. **Molecular features**: CO (4.7 μm), H₂O (weak)
3. **Continuum opacity**: Rayleigh scattering, haze particles

## Nightside Chemistry (Cool Side, ~3800 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Anti-stellar point**: 3800-4000 K
- **Mid-night terminator**: 3600-3800 K
- **Limb on nightside**: 3400-3600 K

### Dominant Molecules

| Species | Dayside | Nightside | Change |
|---------|---------|-----------|--------|
| H₂ | 0.75 | 0.78 | ×1.04 |
| CO | 10⁻⁶ | 10⁻⁵ | ×10 |
| H₂O | <10⁻⁷ | 10⁻⁶ | ×100 |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻² | 10⁻⁴ | ÷100 |

### Thermal Chemistry

**Key recombination reactions**:

1. **Hydrogen recombination**: H + H + M → H₂ + M; τ ~ 100-1000 s
2. **Water formation**: H + OH + M → H₂O + M; τ ~ 10-100 s
3. **Alkali metal recombination**: Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na; τ ~ 10-100 s

### Condensates

**Potential condensate layers**:

1. **Iron (Fe) condensation**: T ~ 1600 K
2. **Forsterite (Mg₂SiO₄)**: T < 1800 K
3. **Trace refractory oxides**: Al₂O₃, TiO₂, VO

## Circulation & Wind Patterns

### Dayside Wind Speeds

- **Equatorial super-rotating winds**: 4-8 km/s
- **Meridional winds**: 1-3 km/s
- **Vertical updrafts**: 10-50 m/s

### Nightside Winds

- **Equatorial winds**: 2-4 km/s eastward
- **Meridional circulation**: Poleward at upper levels
- **Wind shear**: d(v)/dz ~ 0.01-0.1 s⁻¹

### Advection Timescales

- **Planetary circumference**: ~130,000 km
- **Wind speed**: ~5 km/s
- **Circulation time around planet**: 7-10 hours
- **Orbital period**: 1.48 days = 35.5 hours

### Circulation Cell Structure

1. **Equatorial super-rotating jet**: 4-8 km/s, ±30-40° latitude
2. **Mid-latitude cells**: Poleward/equatorward return flow
3. **Polar regions**: Slow circulation with possible stationary vortex

## Observable Consequences for JWST

### Predicted Emission Spectrum

**Dayside emission** (relative to stellar continuum):

- **0.5-1 μm**: Thermal continuum (blackbody at 4600 K)
- **1-2 μm**: Na I lines, thermal emission
- **2-3 μm**: H₂O weak, CO marginal
- **4.7 μm**: CO fundamental band (50-200 ppm)
- **5-10 μm**: CO band dominates

### Predicted Transmission Spectrum

**Key features**:

1. **Rayleigh scattering haze**: τ ~ 0.5-2 (50-400 ppm elevation)
2. **Alkali metal absorption**: Na I (20-100 ppm), K I (10-50 ppm)
3. **Molecular bands**: H₂O <10 ppm, CO <5 ppm

### Phase Curve Predictions

- **Dayside emission (phase 0°)**: T_eff ~ 4600 K
- **Nightside emission (phase 180°)**: T_eff ~ 3800 K
- **Peak emission phase**: ~10-20° eastward (wind offset)

### Secondary Eclipse Depth

| Wavelength | Depth (ppm) | Species |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| 0.6 μm | 150-300 | Continuum + metals |
| 1.5 μm | 250-400 | Continuum |
| 4.7 μm | 300-600 | CO + continuum |
| 12 μm | 100-250 | Thermal only |

### Which Molecules Are Detectable

1. **CO**: 4.7 μm, 300-600 ppm depth, SNR ~5-10σ
2. **H₂O**: 1.4-3.2 μm, ~10⁻⁶ abundance, SNR ~1-2σ
3. **Na and K**: D-lines at 589 nm (SNR ~5-10σ), 769 nm (SNR ~2-3σ)
4. **TiO/VO**: Weak, SNR <1σ

### Best Observation Strategy

1. **Phase curve monitoring**: 9 phases, F444W + F150W, 40-50 hours
2. **Secondary eclipses**: 4.7 μm observations, 3-5 eclipses
3. **Transmission spectroscopy**: NIRSpec G395M + G235M, 10+ transits
4. **Targets**: CO (high), Na/K (high), H₂O (medium)
5. **Conditions**: 3-5 orbital periods for phase coverage

## Summary

KELT-9b is the hottest known exoplanet, with extreme dayside temperatures (4600 K) and massive H/He envelope surrounding a ~15-25 M_⊕ core. The dayside atmosphere exhibits strong photochemical dissociation of water and ionization of alkali metals. The nightside (3800 K) allows molecular recombination. Extreme winds (5-8 km/s) create unique circulation patterns. JWST observations of the CO band at 4.7 μm and Na I lines should provide high-SNR detections, while phase curve measurements constrain atmospheric dynamics.
