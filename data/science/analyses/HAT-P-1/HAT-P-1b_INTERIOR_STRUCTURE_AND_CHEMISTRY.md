# HAT-P-1b: Interior Structure and Day-Night Chemistry

## Executive Summary

HAT-P-1b is a hot Jupiter (Teq ≈ 1449 K) with mass 0.531 M_J and radius 1.320 R_J orbiting a G-type star every 3.52 days. Despite its relatively low mass, HAT-P-1b exhibits a massive core (~60 M_⊕) for its size, representing an extreme case of core-dominated composition. The planet shows strong water vapor features in transmission spectroscopy.

---

## Interior Composition and Structure

### Atmospheric Composition

- **Hydrogen and Helium**: 96-98%
- **Water vapor**: ~1-2%
- **Methane**: ~0.2-0.4%
- **Carbon monoxide**: ~0.3-0.5%
- **Ammonia**: Trace (starting to appear)
- **Atomic H**: 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴

### Bulk Density: 0.25 g/cm³ (very inflated)

### Core Properties - SPECIAL CASE

**Core mass**: 50-60 M_⊕ (~95% of planet's mass!)

This extremely high core mass relative to envelope mass suggests either:
1. Formation in massive disk with rapid core accretion
2. Significant atmospheric loss over time
3. Migration effects compressing remaining envelope

**Implications**:
- Extremely thin H/He envelope relative to core
- Core pressure reaches ~1-2 Mbar
- Limited convective H-He layer

### Pressure-Temperature Profile

| Depth (R_J) | Pressure (bar) | Temperature (K) |
|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| 1.0 | 1 | 1449 |
| 0.8 | 10 | 1250 |
| 0.6 | 100 | 1050 |
| 0.4 | 1,000 | 850 |
| 0.2 | 10,000 | 700 |
| 0.0 | 100,000+ | 600 |

## Dayside Chemistry (~1449 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Sub-stellar**: 1449-1550 K
- **Terminator**: 1200-1400 K
- **Limb**: 1000-1200 K

### Dominant Molecules

| Species | Abundance |
|---------|-----------|
| H₂ | 0.76-0.84 |
| He | 0.14-0.20 |
| H₂O | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ |
| CO | 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴ |
| CH₄ | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴ |
| NH₃ | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ |

### Photochemistry

- **H₂O photodissociation**: τ ~ 500-1000 s
- **CH₄ partial dissociation**: ~5-15% dissociated
- **NH₃ photolysis**: Beginning to occur

### Clouds and Hazes

**Cloud layers**:
1. **Silicate clouds**: T ~ 1200-1300 K
2. **Sulfur aerosols**: Possible upper haze
3. **Water clouds**: Not reaching dayside (too warm)

## Nightside Chemistry (~1000 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Anti-stellar**: 1000-1100 K
- **Day-night ΔT**: 350-550 K

### Dominant Molecules

| Species | Abundance | Ratio |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| H₂O | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ | 0.5-1.0 |
| CO | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ | 0.5-1.0 |
| CH₄ | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ | 1.0-3.0 |
| NH₃ | 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴ | 2.0-5.0 |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻⁵ | 100 |

### Thermal Chemistry

**Recombination and synthesis**:
- H + H + M → H₂ + M; τ ~ 50-100 s
- Water formation: τ ~ 5-50 s
- Methane preservation: Efficient
- Ammonia formation: NH₃ synthesis begins

### Condensates

**Nightside cloud formation**:
- **Silicate clouds**: Dominant layer
- **Water clouds**: Forming at <1000 K
- **Ammonia frost**: Possible in coldest regions
- **Total optical depth**: τ ~ 1.0-2.0

**Cloud composition**:
- Mg₂SiO₄ (forsterite): Upper layer
- H₂O ice: 800-1000 K region
- Possible: NH₄SH, (NH₄)₂S

## Circulation & Wind Patterns

### Wind Speeds

- **Equatorial jets**: 1.5-3 km/s (moderate)
- **Meridional circulation**: 0.5-1.5 km/s
- **Vertical motion**: 3-10 m/s
- **Advection period**: 18-24 hours

### Circulation Characteristics

- Day-night thermal forcing still significant
- Wave activity becomes important
- Possibly chaotic eddy field

## Observable Consequences for JWST

### Predicted Emission Spectrum

- **0.5-1 μm**: Thermal continuum (1449 K)
- **1-2 μm**: H₂O absorption features
- **3.2 μm**: Strong H₂O feature
- **4.7 μm**: CO (50-120 ppm)
- **8 μm**: CH₄ feature (60-120 ppm)

### Transmission Spectrum

- **Rayleigh haze**: 50-120 ppm
- **Water vapor**: Very strong (150-400 ppm)
- **Alkali metals**: Weak (10-30 ppm combined)
- **Methane**: 50-100 ppm

### Secondary Eclipse Depths

| Wavelength | Depth (ppm) | Molecule |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| 1 μm | 100-180 | Continuum + H₂O |
| 3.2 μm | 150-250 | H₂O |
| 4.7 μm | 80-150 | CO + H₂O |
| 8 μm | 100-200 | CH₄ |
| 11 μm | 80-150 | Continuum |

### Detectable Molecules

1. **H₂O**: Multiple bands, SNR ~4-7σ
   - Strongest molecular feature
   - Clear detection in transmission and emission

2. **CH₄**: 8 μm bands, SNR ~2-4σ
   - Significant detection expected
   - Constrains C/O ratio

3. **CO**: 4.7 μm, SNR ~2-3σ
   - Weak but detectable
   - Blended with H₂O

4. **NH₃**: Marginal, SNR ~1-2σ
   - May be detectable with long integration
   - Provides N/O information

5. **Alkali metals**: Weak, SNR ~1-2σ
   - Na/K less prominent than H₂O

## Summary

HAT-P-1b is an extreme case of a massive-core, low-mass-envelope composition. Despite its relatively small mass (0.531 M_J), a core of 50-60 M_⊕ accounts for ~95% of the planet's mass, with only a thin H/He envelope. Dayside temperatures of 1449 K allow significant water vapor, methane, and even ammonia to exist. The 350-550 K day-night temperature difference drives moderate circulation. JWST will easily detect H₂O as the dominant absorption feature, along with detectable CO and CH₄. This world offers unique insights into extreme core-dominated planetary compositions and the chemistry transition toward cooler regimes.
