# WASP-76b: Interior Structure and Day-Night Chemistry

## Executive Summary

WASP-76b is an ultra-hot Jupiter (Teq ≈ 2380 K) with mass 0.92 M_J and radius 1.87 R_J, famous for its extreme iron rain phenomenon. With an orbital period of 1.8 days around an F-type star, WASP-76b exhibits the most dramatic dayside-to-nightside asymmetry known, driven by violent atmospheric circulation and iron condensation.

---

## Interior Composition and Structure

### Atmospheric Composition

- **Hydrogen and Helium**: 96-98%
- **Water vapor**: ~1-2% (heavily photodissociated)
- **Carbon monoxide**: ~0.3-0.5%
- **Iron species**: Fe, Fe⁺, FeO, Fe₂O₃ (trace but dramatic)
- **Atomic species**: H, O, C abundant
- **Alkali metals**: Na, K ionized on dayside

### Bulk Density

**Mean density**: 0.26 g/cm³ (extremely inflated)

**Envelope structure**:
1. **Thermosphere**: Atomic H, ionized metals, plasma
2. **Upper photosphere**: H₂O dissociation, alkali ionization
3. **Middle photosphere**: H₂ dominant, iron vapor zone
4. **Lower envelope**: Cool H₂/He with iron clouds

### Core Properties

**Core mass**: 15-20 M_⊕

**Composition**:
- Iron-nickel center
- Silicate mantle
- Extended high-pressure regimes

### Pressure-Temperature Profile

| 1.0 | 0.5 | 2380 |
| 0.8 | 10 | 2000 |

## Dayside Chemistry (Iron Rain Region, ~2380 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Sub-stellar point**: 2380-2500 K
- **Terminator**: 2000-2200 K
- **Limb**: 1800-2000 K
- **Day-night ΔT**: 800-1200 K

### Dominant Molecules and Iron Chemistry

| Species | Dayside Abundance | Physical State |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| H₂ | 0.76-0.84 | Gas |
| He | 0.14-0.20 | Gas |
| Fe | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Atomic vapor |
| FeO | 10⁻⁷ - 10⁻⁶ | Molecular vapor |
| Fe₂O₃ | <10⁻⁸ | Trace |
| CO | 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴ | Molecular |
| H₂O | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Vapor |
| Na⁺/K⁺ | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Ionized |

### Photochemistry

**XUV-driven processes**:
- **H₂O photodissociation**: τ ~ 100-200 s
- **Iron vaporization**: Atomic Fe in upper atmosphere
- **OH formation**: H₂O photolysis produces OH radicals
- **Iron oxide formation**: FeO + hν → Fe + O (dissociation)

### Iron Rain Phenomenon

**Dayside iron circulation**:

1. **Upper atmosphere (>1 mbar)**:
   - Temperature: 2300-2500 K
   - Iron exists as atomic vapor (Fe)
   - Ionized species: Fe⁺, Fe²⁺
   - Iron abundance: 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁶ (ppm levels)

2. **Cloud formation zones (1-100 mbar)**:
   - Temperature: 1800-2200 K
   - Iron condensation threshold: ~1600-1800 K
   - Formation of iron droplets: Fe(l)
   - Settling timescale: 10-100 hours to lower levels

3. **Wind circulation**:
   - Equatorial eastward winds: 5-7 km/s
   - Iron-bearing aerosols transported poleward
   - Advection to nightside: ~10-20 hours

### Haze and Cloud Formation

**Dayside condensate layers**:

1. **Iron clouds**: T ~ 1600-1800 K
   - Particle composition: Metallic iron droplets
   - Particle size: 1-10 μm
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.1-0.3

2. **Silicate clouds**: T < 1600 K
   - Mg₂SiO₄, MgSiO₃ formation
   - Altitude: 10-100 bar region

3. **TiO/VO hazes**: Upper photosphere
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.2-0.5
   - Composition: Titanium/vanadium oxides

### Spectroscopic Features

**Visible/NIR features**:
- **Iron absorption**: Continuum opacity from Fe droplets at 0.5-2 μm
- **TiO bands**: Absorption at 0.6-1 μm
- **Alkali metals**: Na (589 nm), K (769 nm) strong

**Mid-IR features**:
- **CO fundamental**: 4.7 μm emission
- **Silicate features**: 10 μm absorption possible
- **Iron continuum**: Broad absorption across 2-10 μm

## Nightside Chemistry (Cool Side with Iron Snow, ~1200 K)

### Temperature Profile

- **Anti-stellar point**: 1200-1300 K
- **Evening terminator**: 1500-1700 K
- **Morning terminator**: 1400-1600 K
- **Extreme asymmetry**: Evening ~500-700 K cooler than morning

### Dominant Molecules

| Species | Abundance | Formation |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| H₂ | 0.78-0.82 | Recombined from H |
| CO | 10⁻⁴ - 10⁻³ | Stabilized |
| H₂O | 10⁻⁵ - 10⁻⁴ | Recombination |
| Fe | 10⁻⁸ - 10⁻⁷ | Evaporating from clouds |
| FeO | 10⁻⁸ - 10⁻⁷ | Oxide formation |
| H (atomic) | 10⁻⁶ - 10⁻⁵ | Residual dissociation |

### Iron Snow Formation

**Nightside iron condensation**:

1. **Condensation cascade** (cooling from day to night):
   - 2000 K → 1800 K: Iron droplets form
   - 1800 K → 1600 K: FeO formation, iron oxide snow
   - 1600 K → 1400 K: Additional iron oxide accumulation
   - <1400 K: Iron oxide/silicate mixture

2. **Particle composition**:
   - Primary: Metallic iron (Fe)
   - Secondary: Iron oxide (FeO, Fe₂O₃)
   - Tertiary: Iron silicates (FeSiO₃)
   - Mixture particles with 50-200 μm size

3. **Optical properties**:
   - Optical depth: τ_iron ~ 0.5-2.0
   - Reflectivity: High in visible (iron mirrors photons)
   - Emissivity: Low in thermal IR

### Cloud and Condensate Structure

**Nightside layer structure**:

1. **Upper cloud deck (1-10 mbar)**:
   - Composition: Iron oxide aerosols (small particles)
   - Temperature: 1400-1600 K
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.3-0.5

2. **Middle cloud deck (10-100 mbar)**:
   - Composition: Mixed iron and silicates
   - Temperature: 1200-1400 K
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.5-1.0

3. **Lower cloud deck (100-1000 mbar)**:
   - Composition: Large iron particles settling
   - Temperature: 1000-1200 K
   - Optical depth: τ ~ 0.3-0.5

**Total cloud optical depth on nightside**: τ_total ~ 1.0-2.0 (reflecting away starlight)

### Day-Night Molecular Abundance Ratios

| Molecule | Day/Night Ratio | Physical Reason |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Fe | 100 | Vapor on hot dayside; condensed on cool nightside |
| FeO | 100 | Oxide formation only in cooler regions |
| H (atomic) | 100 | Photodissociation vs. recombination |
| CO | 0.1 | Stabilization on cool side |
| H₂O | 0.2 | Recombination dominates |

### Recombination Chemistry

**Thermal chemistry timescales**:

1. **Hydrogen recombination**: H + H + M → H₂ + M
   - Rate: k ≈ 10⁻³² cm⁶ s⁻¹
   - Timescale: τ ~ 100-1000 s

2. **Water formation**: H + OH + M → H₂O + M
   - Rate: k ≈ 10⁻³⁰ cm⁶ s⁻¹
   - Timescale: τ ~ 10-100 s

3. **Iron oxide formation**: Fe + O₂ → FeO + O (simplified)
   - Rate: Extremely fast at nightside temps
   - Timescale: <1 s (limited by transport)

## Circulation & Wind Patterns

### Dayside Wind Speeds

- **Equatorial super-rotation**: 5-7 km/s (strong)
- **Meridional component**: 1-3 km/s
- **Vertical updrafts**: 10-30 m/s (vigorous)
- **Characteristic timescale**: ~6 hours for equatorial transit

### Nightside Winds

- **Equatorial flow**: 3-5 km/s (slower than dayside)
- **Meridional circulation**: Weak poleward at upper levels
- **Descending motion**: Slow sinking over anti-stellar region

### Advection Timescales

- **Circumplanetary transport**: 8-12 hours
- **Vertical advection**: 5-20 hours (iron settling)
- **Full day-night circulation**: 18-24 hours

### Large-Scale Circulation

**Asymmetric 3-cell pattern**:

1. **Primary dayside cell**: Rising air at sub-stellar point
2. **Nightside descent**: Cool, iron-laden air sinking
3. **Poleward jets**: Strong wind shear at terminators

**Rossby number**: Ro ~ 0.1-0.5 (Coriolis important)

## Observable Consequences for JWST

### Predicted Emission Spectrum

**Dayside** (hot side):
- **0.5-1 μm**: Thermal continuum (2380 K), darkened by iron clouds
- **1-2 μm**: Reduced brightness (iron absorption)
- **4.7 μm**: CO fundamental (100-200 ppm)
- **Brightness**: Dimmer than typical hot Jupiter due to iron clouds

**Nightside** (cool side):
- **0.5-1 μm**: Darker (1200 K equivalent, reflecting iron clouds)
- **4.7 μm**: Weaker emission (cooler temperature)
- **Thermal contrast**: Night significantly dimmer than day

### Phase Curve Behavior

**Asymmetric phase curve**:
- **Phase 0° to +90°** (dayside): High emission, declining toward terminator
- **Phase +90° to +180°** (nightside): Low emission, reflecting iron clouds
- **Phase +180° to +270°**: Gradual brightening toward evening terminator
- **Phase +270° to 360°**: Rising toward dayside

**Expected phase curve depth**: 500-1000 ppm (one of most asymmetric known)

### Secondary Eclipse Depth

| Wavelength | Dayside Depth (ppm) | Reason |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| 0.5 μm | 100-200 | Reduced by iron absorption |
| 1.5 μm | 150-300 | Iron continuum opacity |
| 4.7 μm | 200-400 | CO + thermal + iron |
| 11 μm | 100-250 | Thermal only |

**Nightside thermal emission**: ~50-100 ppm (extremely faint)

### Predicted Transmission Spectrum

**Key features**:

1. **Rayleigh haze**: τ ~ 0.5-1.5 (100-300 ppm elevation)
2. **Alkali metals**: Na (50-150 ppm), K (20-50 ppm)
3. **Iron absorption**: Continuum elevation at short wavelengths
4. **Molecular bands**: Weak CO <20 ppm, weak H₂O <10 ppm

### Which Molecules Are Detectable

1. **CO**: 4.7 μm, 200-400 ppm, SNR ~3-5σ
2. **H₂O**: 2-3 μm, SNR ~1-2σ (marginal)
3. **Na**: 589 nm, 50-150 ppm, SNR ~4-6σ
4. **Iron features**: Broad continuum in visible, SNR ~5-10σ
5. **Fe absorption**: 0.5-2 μm, distinctive feature

### Best Observation Strategy

1. **Phase curve observations**:
   - Full orbital coverage (9-12 points)
   - F444W (4.4 μm) for CO detection
   - F200W (2.0 μm) for iron/continuum
   - 40-50 hours program time

2. **Transmission spectroscopy**:
   - NIRSpec G235M for Na/K detection
   - NIRSpec G395M for CO structure
   - Multiple transits for high SNR

3. **Specific targets**:
   - **High priority**: Phase curve (asymmetry), iron features
   - **Medium priority**: CO detection, Na/K lines
   - **Low priority**: H₂O bands (weak signal)

4. **Critical measurements**:
   - Evening vs. morning terminator asymmetry
   - Iron cloud optical depth determination
   - Nightside temperature measurement
   - Phase offset from wind circulation

## Summary

WASP-76b is famous for its dramatic iron rain phenomenon, where iron vaporized on the hot dayside (2380 K) condenses into iron oxide snow on the cool nightside (1200 K). The extreme 1200 K day-night temperature difference creates violent circulation patterns, with iron-laden winds carrying metal from the dayside to the nightside. Large iron clouds form on the nightside, creating the most asymmetric planetary emission spectrum known. JWST observations can map the iron condensation process, detect CO in both day and night sides, and measure the extraordinary phase curve asymmetry. This world represents a unique laboratory for studying condensation chemistry in ultra-hot atmospheres.
