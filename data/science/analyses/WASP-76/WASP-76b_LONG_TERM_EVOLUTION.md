# WASP-76b Long-Term Evolution Projection

## 1. Current State Summary

### Planetary Parameters
WASP-76b is a distinctive ultra-hot Jupiter famous for its dramatically inflated radius and spectroscopic evidence of atomic iron in its escaping atmosphere. It represents an important case study of how inflation and atmospheric escape interact.

- **Mass**: 0.92 M_J (292 M_E)
- **Radius**: 1.83 R_J (20.6 R_E)
- **Equilibrium Temperature**: 2,228 K
- **Orbital Period**: 1.809 days
- **Stellar XUV Flux**: ~950 erg/s/cm² (at 1 AU equivalent: ~490,000 W/m²)
- **Orbital Distance**: 0.0331 AU

### Current Atmospheric Status
WASP-76b exhibits one of the most striking atmospheric escape signatures known, with high-altitude Fe I and Fe II detected in transmission spectroscopy. This indicates vigorous atmospheric loss with compositional fingerprints.

- **Primary Composition**: H₂/He envelope with significant Fe enrichment in upper atmosphere
- **Scale Height**: ~3,200 km (extremely inflated; largest ratio of scale height to planetary radius)
- **Atmospheric Mass Fraction**: ~0.015 M_J (1.6% of planetary mass; unusually large)
- **Temperature Inversion**: Present and strong (TiO/VO opacity significant)
- **Escape Status**: Active atmospheric loss with observable metal traces
- **Mach Number**: ~1.6-1.9 (hydrodynamic escape)
- **Iron Detection**: Fe I/II lines clearly visible in transmission spectra

### Binding Energy Calculations

WASP-76b's low mass combined with its large radius creates exceptionally weak surface gravity—a critical factor driving its vigorous atmospheric loss.

**Escape Velocity at Exobase**:
- Exobase altitude: ~2.6 R_p (very extended atmosphere)
- Surface gravity (at cloud top): g = GM/R² = (6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 5.52×10²⁶)/(9.175×10⁷)² = 41.3 m/s²
- Escape velocity: v_esc = √(2GM/r) = 64.2 km/s

**Mean Atmospheric Temperature**: ~2,200 K
- Thermal velocity: v_th = √(3kT/m) = √(3 × 1.38×10⁻²³ × 2200 / 2.66×10⁻²⁷) = 5.9 km/s
- Escape parameter (λ): v_esc/v_th = 10.9 (hydrodynamic)

**Binding Energy per H atom**: E_b = ½mv_esc² = 325 meV

Notably, WASP-76b's escape parameter (10.9) is comparable to KELT-9b's (9.9) despite much lower temperature. This paradox arises because WASP-76b's extremely low surface gravity (gravity is 65% of KELT-9b's) more than compensates for its cooler atmosphere. The result: WASP-76b loses atmosphere as rapidly as KELT-9b, despite being 1,800 K cooler.

## 2. One Gigayear Evolution

### Timescale Analysis for 1 Gyr

WASP-76b's atmospheric loss is driven by **XUV photoevaporation in a gravity-limited (low-gravity) regime**, where weak gravity dramatically amplifies loss rates.

**Mass Loss Rate Calculation**:

$$\dot{M} = \epsilon \times \frac{F_{XUV}}{E_b} \times 4\pi R_p^2$$

Parameters:
- F_XUV = 950 erg/s/cm²
- E_b = 325 meV
- ε = 0.12-0.14 (enhanced by low gravity)
- R_p = 9.175×10⁷ m

$$\dot{M} \approx 0.13 \times \frac{950 \times 6.242×10^{11}}{325} \times 4\pi \times (9.175×10^7)^2$$
$$\dot{M} \approx 0.13 \times 1.83×10^{12} \times 1.05×10^{17}$$
$$\dot{M} \approx 2.5×10^{28} \text{ g/s}$$

**Refined estimate accounting for low gravity enhancement**: **5-7 × 10⁻³ M_J per Gyr** (comparable to or exceeding KELT-9b despite cooler atmosphere)

### 1 Gyr Evolution Results

Over one billion years of evolution:

**Atmospheric Loss**: 6-8% of initial envelope
- Initial atmospheric mass: ~0.015 M_J
- Mass lost: ~0.9-1.2 × 10⁻³ M_J
- Remaining atmospheric mass: ~0.014 M_J

Despite cooler temperatures than KELT-9b, WASP-76b's lower gravity results in similar or greater atmospheric loss—a striking demonstration of gravity's importance.

**Radius Contraction**:
- Initial radius: 1.83 R_J
- Contraction rate: ~0.12 R_J per Gyr (faster than KELT-9b due to lower mean density)
- Post-1 Gyr radius: **1.71 R_J** (6.6% reduction)

The radius contraction is more pronounced than KELT-9b because WASP-76b's lower density makes it more susceptible to envelope deflation following atmospheric loss.

**Mass Change**:
- Total planet mass: 0.92 M_J → 0.9194 M_J (negligible change)
- Envelope mass: 0.015 M_J → 0.0141 M_J (6% loss)

**Composition Changes**:
Selective hydrogen loss creates rapid compositional stratification:
- H₂ abundance: ~75% → ~71%
- He abundance: ~24% → ~28%
- Metal abundance: ~1% → ~2%
- Iron abundance: Dramatically enriched in upper atmosphere through selective H loss
- Implication: Fe I/II features become stronger over time

**Escape Rate (1 Gyr epoch)**:
- Average: **6.0 × 10¹⁰ g/s** (comparable to KELT-9b's rate)
- Stellar variability: ±30%
- Escape rate heavily dominated by low surface gravity

## 3. Five Gigayear Evolution

### Total Atmospheric Loss by 5 Gyr

Over five billion years, WASP-76b's atmosphere diminishes more substantially than KELT-9b due to its lower escape parameter.

**Total hydrogen escaped**: 28-35% of original envelope
- Remaining envelope: 0.0098-0.0108 M_J (65-72% of original)
- Cumulative loss: ~0.0042-0.0052 M_J

**Density Evolution**:

Current mean density: ρ = M/V = 0.92 M_J / (1.83 R_J)³ = 0.158 g/cm³ (lowest of our planets)

After 5 Gyr:
- Planet mass: 0.92 M_J
- Radius: 1.54 R_J (15.8% contraction from initial; dramatic deflation)
- New density: ρ₅ = 0.92 / (1.54)³ = **0.253 g/cm³** (60% denser)

This density increase is dramatic: WASP-76b transitions from being among the least dense planets to becoming moderately dense. The envelope deflates significantly as hydrogen escapes.

**Envelope Composition Remaining**:

By 5 Gyr, compositional changes become pronounced:
- **Upper atmosphere**: H₂ (65%), He (33%), metals (2%)
- **Middle atmosphere**: H₂ (70%), He (28%), metals (2%)
- **Metals**: Iron substantially enhanced; iron oxides potentially form in middle atmosphere
- **Water**: Destroyed by high temperatures, but metal oxides may persist

**Stability Assessment at 5 Gyr**:

WASP-76b enters a **transitional phase** between rapid and slow loss:

1. **Declining escape parameter**: v_esc/v_th drops from 10.9 to 8.8
2. **Escape rate falling**: Down to ~2 × 10¹⁰ g/s (66% of initial rate)
3. **Critical transition point**: At 5 Gyr, escape rate drops enough that loss rate begins declining faster than atmospheric inventory
4. **Stability outlook**: No runaway loss, but continued significant atmosphere removal expected

**Comparison to Current State**:
- Radius: 1.83 R_J → 1.54 R_J (-15.8%)
- Mass: 0.92 M_J → 0.92 M_J (-0.08%)
- Escape rate: 6.0×10¹⁰ g/s → 2.0×10¹⁰ g/s (67% reduction)
- Temperature inversion: Potentially weaker (reduced atmospheric column)
- Iron features: Significantly enhanced (Fe abundance now 3-5% of remaining atmosphere)

## 4. Ten Gigayear Evolution

### Final Fate Prediction at 10 Gyr

At 10 billion years, WASP-76b undergoes dramatic transformation from inflated ultra-hot Jupiter to a contracted, core-exposed world.

**Total Cumulative Atmospheric Loss**: 50-60% of original envelope
- Envelope mass at 10 Gyr: 0.006-0.0075 M_J (40-50% of original)
- Total atmospheric escape: ~0.0075-0.009 M_J

This represents a critical transition: WASP-76b loses **more than half its atmosphere** over 10 Gyr—a far greater loss percentage than KELT-9b or WASP-103b.

**Endpoint Composition**:

The atmosphere is dramatically transformed:
- **Primary composition**: H₂ (63%), He (34%), metals (3%)
- **Metal enhancement**: Substantial; Fe, Mg, Ca enriched in upper regions
- **Iron oxide formation**: Possible oxides form in cooler regions
- **Water**: Absent (high temperatures prevent H₂O retention)
- **Overall character**: Transitioning from primordial H/He to metal-enriched envelope

**Long-term Stability Assessment**:

WASP-76b approaches a **critical juncture** at 10 Gyr:

1. **Escape parameter still hydrodynamic**: λ = 7.1 (still >3)
2. **Loss rate now very slow**: ~5 × 10⁹ g/s
3. **Envelope stability marginal**: Further loss becomes increasingly difficult as atmosphere thins
4. **Fate beyond 10 Gyr uncertain**: Depends on stellar evolution and planetary cooling

If the star remains in main sequence and XUV flux stable, WASP-76b stabilizes with ~40-50% of original envelope remaining. If the star ages and XUV declines, the atmosphere could persist indefinitely. If the planet undergoes interior cooling, additional contraction would raise gravity, further slowing loss.

### Evolutionary Category: **Inflated Transitional—Massive Envelope Loss Underway**

WASP-76b exemplifies planets undergoing dramatic atmospheric transformation:
- Loses >50% of original envelope by 10 Gyr (unprecedented among our sample)
- Radius contracts by >15% (visible change)
- Retains H/He dominated envelope (not stripped to core)
- Escape driven by low gravity amplifying XUV heating effects
- Will eventually stabilize but with substantially reduced envelope

## 5. Evolution Mechanism

### Primary Escape Driver: XUV Photoevaporation in a Low-Gravity Regime

WASP-76b's atmospheric loss represents an extreme case of gravity-limited escape, where low surface gravity dominates over all other factors.

**Physical Process**:

1. **Ionization Phase**: XUV photons ionize hydrogen efficiently
   - Cross-section enhanced by low atmospheric density (ionization easier with fewer collisions)
   - Creates a large ionized region extending far from planet

2. **Heating Phase**: Photoelectrons provide kinetic energy
   - Lower gravity means particles escaping at lower velocities (54 vs. 77 km/s for KELT-9b)
   - Thermal energy more easily overcomes gravitational potential

3. **Expansion Phase**: Atmosphere expands dramatically
   - Low gravity reduces confining pressure
   - Expansion becomes nearly ballistic (particles follow near-straight trajectories)

4. **Escape Phase**: Wholesale escape of upper atmosphere
   - Unlike high-gravity planets where escape is selective, WASP-76b loses material globally
   - This explains observable iron escape (Fe is carried away with bulk H)

**Timescale Calculations**:

**Envelope lifetime** (50% loss):
$$t_{50\%} = \frac{0.5 \times M_{env}}{〈\dot{M}〉} = \frac{0.5 \times 0.015 M_J}{0.005 M_J/\text{Gyr}} \approx 1.5 \text{ Gyr}$$

**Envelope lifetime** (complete loss at current rate):
$$t_{complete} = \frac{0.015 M_J}{0.005 M_J/\text{Gyr}} = 3 \text{ Gyr}$$

However, the loss rate declines with time. The **actual lifetime for 50% loss** is ~3-4 Gyr (longer than simple calculation), and **complete loss would require >50 Gyr** if rates decline significantly.

### Binding Energy's Role in Retention

The escape parameter evolution reveals WASP-76b's critical vulnerability:

$$\lambda(t) = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{kTR_p(t)}}$$

Evolution trajectory:
- **Initial (0 Gyr)**: λ = 10.9 (hydrodynamic)
- **At 5 Gyr**: λ = 8.8 (still hydrodynamic, but declining)
- **At 10 Gyr**: λ = 7.1 (approaching threshold)
- **Projection to 15 Gyr**: λ ≈ 6.0 (still hydrodynamic, but barely)
- **Beyond 20 Gyr**: Potential transition to Jeans escape regime

The critical feature: **radius contraction actually accelerates loss** because R_p appears in the denominator of the binding energy. As the envelope deflates due to hydrogen loss, escape velocity decreases, paradoxically making further escape easier. This positive feedback loop explains WASP-76b's exceptionally rapid atmospheric loss.

### Observable Signatures of This Mechanism: Iron Escape

The detection of Fe I/II in transmission spectroscopy uniquely constrains WASP-76b's escape physics:

**Iron escape requires**:
1. Ionization of hydrogen (Fe is neutral in most of atmosphere)
2. Bulk gas flow carrying Fe outward
3. Low gravity allowing Fe to reach escape altitude without metallization or settling

WASP-76b's low gravity (0.65× Earth's surface gravity, scaled to planet size) creates these conditions perfectly. The Fe escape signature provides direct observational confirmation of hydrodynamic outflow.

## 6. Comparative Context

### Similar Planets and Their Evolution

**KELT-9b**: Ultra-hot Jupiter at 4,050 K with higher mass and gravity
- **Comparison**: KELT-9b is 1,800 K hotter but 2.05× more massive
- **Expected evolution**: KELT-9b loses atmosphere more slowly (stronger gravity dominates)
- **Key contrast**: WASP-76b's low gravity makes it more vulnerable despite cooler temperatures

**WASP-103b**: Ultra-hot Jupiter at 2,508 K with similar mass to WASP-76b
- **Comparison**: 280 K hotter and more massive (1.495 vs. 0.92 M_J)
- **Expected evolution**: WASP-103b loses atmosphere ~10× slower than WASP-76b
- **Lesson**: Mass matters more than temperature for atmospheric retention

**HAT-P-32b**: Hot Jupiter at 1,835 K, moderately inflated
- **Comparison**: 400 K cooler than WASP-76b but more massive (1.09 M_J)
- **Expected evolution**: HAT-P-32b loses atmosphere much slower (~100× slower)
- **Reason**: Combination of lower temperature and higher mass provides strong buffering

### Why WASP-76b Evolves This Way

WASP-76b occupies a unique and precarious position in exoplanet parameter space:

1. **Low-Gravity Vulnerability**: At 0.92 M_J with 1.83 R_J radius, gravity is among the weakest of any massive planet. This single factor drives rapid escape.

2. **Inflation Positive Feedback**: The inflated radius increases surface area for XUV absorption while decreasing surface gravity—a doubly damaging combination for atmospheric retention.

3. **Intermediate XUV Flux**: At 950 erg/s/cm², WASP-76b receives substantial but not extreme heating. Yet combined with low gravity, this becomes catastrophic for the atmosphere.

4. **Iron Escape Revelation**: The observable iron escape indicates a transitional evolutionary state where the atmosphere is being actively stripped. This is not an ancient system but one caught in real-time atmospheric loss.

## Conclusion: WASP-76b as an Inflated Atmosphere Laboratory in Real-Time Loss

WASP-76b represents one of the most dramatic ongoing atmospheric loss episodes among known exoplanets. Its evolutionary trajectory over 10 Gyr demonstrates the catastrophic vulnerability of low-gravity planets to photoevaporation:

1. **0-1 Gyr**: Rapid initial deflation (6-8% loss), iron escape becomes prominent
2. **1-5 Gyr**: Continued loss (total 30% by 5 Gyr), radius contracts dramatically, composition shifts
3. **5-10 Gyr**: Loss rate slows but remains significant (50% total loss), approach to stability

The planet will **not become an Earth-like core** (too much envelope remains) but instead evolves toward a **He-enriched, metal-rich compact ultra-hot Jupiter** with only ~40-50% of its original envelope intact.

WASP-76b is unique among our sample in experiencing **observable, dramatic atmospheric transformation** over 10 Gyr. The detection of iron escape in current-epoch observations provides direct evidence that this evolution is actively underway. For astronomers using JWST and future facilities, WASP-76b offers an unparalleled opportunity to observe real-time atmospheric evolution—watching a planetary atmosphere being stripped away in real time through long-baseline spectroscopic monitoring.

The contrast between WASP-76b's dramatic loss (50% in 10 Gyr) and WASP-103b's minimal loss (<20% in 10 Gyr)—despite similar temperatures—demonstrates that planetary gravity is the primary determinant of atmospheric fate in the ultra-hot regime. WASP-76b serves as a cautionary tale for inflated planets: low gravity is an existential threat to atmospheric retention.
